# Sleep Disorders and Sleep Deprivation: Clinical Reference Guide
## Source: NCBI Bookshelf — Institute of Medicine (US), Committee on Sleep Medicine and Research

---

## Chapter Summary

It is estimated that 50 to 70 million Americans chronically suffer from a disorder of sleep and wakefulness, hindering daily functioning and adversely affecting health and longevity. There are around 90 distinct sleep disorders; most are marked by one of these symptoms: excessive daytime sleepiness, difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep, and abnormal events occurring during sleep.

The cumulative long-term effects of sleep loss and sleep disorders have been associated with a wide range of deleterious health consequences including an increased risk of hypertension, diabetes, obesity, depression, heart attack, and stroke.

---

## 1. SLEEP LOSS

### Manifestations and Prevalence

Sleep loss generally, in adults, refers to sleep of shorter duration than the average basal need of 7 to 8 hours per night. The main symptom of sleep loss is excessive daytime sleepiness, but other symptoms include depressed mood and poor memory or concentration.

Sleep loss is a highly prevalent problem that continues to worsen in frequency as individuals grow older. Recent studies find that at least 18 percent of adults report receiving insufficient sleep. The percentage of men and women who sleep less than 6 hours has increased significantly over the last 20 years.

### Key Facts
- Optimal sleep duration for adults: 7-8 hours per night
- Optimal sleep for adolescents: ~9 hours per night
- 18%+ of adults report insufficient sleep
- Only 15% of adolescents report sleeping ≥8.5 hours on school nights

## 2. SLEEP-DISORDERED BREATHING (OSA)

### Definition
Sleep-disordered breathing refers to a spectrum of disorders that feature breathing pauses during sleep. The most common disorder is characterized by obstructive apneas and hypopneas, where repeated episodes of collapse (apneas) or partial collapse of the pharyngeal airway occur, usually a result of obstruction by soft tissue in the rear of the throat.

### Pathophysiology
Snoring, which is produced by vibrations of the soft tissues, is a good marker for OSA. Apneas or hypopneas typically result in abrupt and intermittent reduction in blood oxygen saturation, which leads to sleep arousal, often accompanied by loud snorts or gasps as breathing resumes.

Episodic interruptions of breathing also frequently cause cortical and brainstem arousals, interrupting sleep continuity, reducing sleep time, and causing increased sympathetic nervous system activation. These broad systemic effects may contribute to:
- Hypertension
- Coronary artery disease
- Congestive heart failure
- Arrhythmias
- Stroke
- Glucose intolerance and diabetes

### Diagnosis
The diagnosis of OSA requires detection, by polysomnography, of at least five or more apneas or hypopneas per hour of sleep. This rate is expressed as the **apnea-hypopnea index (AHI)** or respiratory disturbance index.

### Prevalence
- **4% of men** and **2% of women** in the middle-aged workforce (with daytime sleepiness requirement)
- **24% of men** and **9% of women** (AHI ≥5, without sleepiness requirement)
- 80-90% of adults with clinically significant sleep-disordered breathing remain undiagnosed

### Treatment: CPAP
Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) is the gold standard treatment:
- Pneumatic splint maintaining upper airway patency during sleep
- Demonstrated to reduce apneas, improve oxygen saturation
- Reduces daytime sleepiness and improves quality of life
- Long-term adherence remains a challenge (50-80% compliance)

## 3. INSOMNIA

### Definition
Insomnia is the most commonly reported sleep problem. It is defined by having difficulty falling asleep, maintaining sleep, or by short sleep duration, despite adequate opportunity for a full night's sleep.

### Diagnostic Criteria (Primary Insomnia)
1. Difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep or nonrestorative sleep
2. Causing clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning
3. Not occurring exclusively during the course of another sleep disorder
4. Not due to the direct physiological effects of a substance or a medical condition

### Prevalence
- Insomnia symptoms affect at least **10% of adults** in the United States
- Higher prevalence among **women** and **older individuals**
- Severe insomnia tends to be chronic — about **85%** of patients continue to report symptoms months or years after diagnosis

### Treatment
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)** — first-line treatment
- **Pharmacotherapy** — benzodiazepine receptor agonists, melatonin receptor agonists
- **Sleep hygiene education**

## 4. NARCOLEPSY AND HYPERSOMNIA

### Definition
Narcolepsy and idiopathic hypersomnia are characterized by a clinically significant complaint of excessive daytime sleepiness that is neither explained by a circadian sleep disorder, sleep-disordered breathing, or sleep deprivation.

### Key Symptoms
1. **Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS)** — constant sleepiness with sleep attacks leading to unintended napping
2. **Cataplexy** — sudden and brief bilateral episodes of muscle weakness triggered by strong emotional stimulus (e.g., laughing)
3. **Sleep paralysis** — muscle paralysis akin to REM sleep atonia while awake
4. **Hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations** — dreamlike REM sleep experiences when falling asleep or waking up
5. **Insomnia** — typically difficulty maintaining sleep
6. **REM behavior disorder** — excessive motor activity during REM sleep

### Diagnosis
Based principally on the **Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT)**, which objectively quantifies daytime sleepiness with 5 daytime naps at 2-hour intervals.

### Key Facts
- Onset often during **mid to late teenage years**
- **Diagnostic delays of 15 to 25 years** are common after symptom onset
- Prevalence: approximately **1 in 2,000** individuals

### Treatment
- **Wakefulness-promoting agents** (modafinil, armodafinil)
- **Sodium oxybate** — for cataplexy and EDS
- **Scheduled naps** — short naps can be refreshing

## 5. PARASOMNIAS

### Definition
Parasomnias are unpleasant or undesirable behaviors or experiences that occur during entry into sleep, during sleep, or during arousals from sleep.

### Classification
- **Primary parasomnias** — predominantly occur during the sleep state
  - NREM parasomnias: Sleepwalking, Sleep terrors, Confusional arousals
  - REM parasomnias: REM behavior disorder, Nightmare disorder
- **Secondary parasomnias** — complications associated with disorders of organ systems during sleep

### Key Features
- Typically manifest during transition periods from one state of sleep to another
- Activities can be potentially **violent or injurious**
- Disruptive to other household members
- May result in excessive daytime sleepiness

## 6. RESTLESS LEGS SYNDROME (RLS)

### Definition
RLS is a neurological condition characterized by an irresistible urge to move the legs (may also affect arms, trunk, or head/neck). Associated with paresthesias — uncomfortable feelings described as creepy-crawly, jittery, itchy, or burning.

### Diagnostic Criteria
1. Urge to move legs, usually accompanied by uncomfortable sensations
2. Symptoms worsen during rest or inactivity
3. Symptoms improve with movement
4. Symptoms worse in evening/night

### Prevalence
- At least **5% of the general population**
- More common in **older adults** and **females**
- Affects over **20% of pregnant women** (transiently)
- One of the most common movement and sleep disorders

### Etiology
- Likely results from **altered dopamine and iron metabolism**
- **Genetic contribution** — >50% of idiopathic cases have positive family history
- Autosomal dominant inheritance with high penetrance (90-100%)
- Susceptibility gene loci on chromosomes 12q, 14q, and 9p

### Association with ADHD
ADHD symptoms were almost twice as likely to occur with symptoms of RLS as would be expected by chance alone.

### Treatment
- **Dopaminergic agents** (pramipexole, ropinirole)
- **Iron supplementation** (if ferritin <75 μg/L)
- **Alpha-2-delta ligands** (gabapentin enacarbil, pregabalin)

## 7. SLEEP AND NEUROLOGICAL DISORDERS

### Overview
Individuals suffering from dementia commonly experience sleep abnormalities. Common patterns across dementias include:
- More **fragmented sleep** leading to more awakenings
- Less total time asleep
- **Decreased REM sleep**
- Sleep impairments usually **worsen as the disease progresses**

### Alzheimer's Disease
- Sleep disturbances occur in up to **45%** of patients
- Sundowning — increased confusion, agitation, and wandering in late afternoon/evening
- Disrupted circadian rhythms
- Higher risk of OSA

### Parkinson's Disease
- Sleep disturbances in **60-98%** of patients
- REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD) — may precede motor symptoms by years
- Excessive daytime sleepiness
- Restless legs syndrome

### Epilepsy
- Seizures can disrupt sleep architecture
- Sleep deprivation can **lower seizure threshold**
- Antiepileptic medications may affect sleep

### Stroke
- Sleep-disordered breathing (OSA) is both a **risk factor for** and **consequence of** stroke
- Post-stroke insomnia is common
- Sleep quality affects stroke recovery and rehabilitation
