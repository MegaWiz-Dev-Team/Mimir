## Sources: AAO-HNS, RCOT, WHO, PubMed Central

## 1. ALLERGIC RHINITIS (โรคจมูกอักเสบภูมิแพ้)

### Definition
Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammatory disease of the nasal mucosa caused by exposure to allergens. It is characterized by nasal congestion, rhinorrhea (runny nose), sneezing, and nasal itching.

### Classification (ARIA Guidelines)
- **Intermittent**: Symptoms <4 days/week or <4 consecutive weeks
- **Persistent**: Symptoms ≥4 days/week and ≥4 consecutive weeks
- **Mild**: Normal sleep, normal daily activities, normal work/school, symptoms not troublesome
- **Moderate-Severe**: Abnormal sleep, impairment of daily activities, or symptoms are troublesome

### Prevalence
- Affects **10-30% of adults** and up to **40% of children** worldwide
- In Thailand: estimated **20-30%** of the adult population
- Co-exists with asthma in 15-38% of patients
- Undertreated in many countries

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical history** — seasonal vs. perennial symptoms, triggers
- **Nasal examination** — pale, swollen turbinates, watery discharge
- **Skin prick test (SPT)** — gold standard for allergen identification
- **Serum specific IgE** — alternative when SPT is not feasible
- **Nasal cytology** — eosinophil count

### Treatment Guidelines
1. **Allergen avoidance** — environmental control measures
2. **Intranasal corticosteroids** — first-line pharmacotherapy (fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide)
3. **Oral antihistamines** — second-generation preferred (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine)
4. **Intranasal antihistamines** — azelastine
5. **Leukotriene receptor antagonists** — montelukast
6. **Allergen immunotherapy (AIT)** — for moderate-severe persistent cases
   - Subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT) routes
7. **Nasal irrigation** — saline lavage as adjunct therapy

## 2. CHRONIC RHINOSINUSITIS (CRS)

### Definition
Inflammation of the nose and paranasal sinuses lasting ≥12 weeks, characterized by:
- Nasal obstruction/congestion
- Nasal discharge (anterior/posterior)
- Facial pain/pressure
- Reduction/loss of smell (hyposmia/anosmia)

### Subtypes
- **CRS without Nasal Polyps (CRSsNP)** — Type 1 inflammation dominant
- **CRS with Nasal Polyps (CRSwNP)** — Type 2 inflammation, eosinophilic
- **Allergic Fungal Rhinosinusitis (AFRS)** — IgE-mediated fungal sensitivity

### Prevalence
- **12% of the US population** (37 million people affected annually)
- One of the most common chronic diseases in adults

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical criteria** — ≥2 cardinal symptoms for ≥12 weeks
- **Nasal endoscopy** — mucosal edema, polyps, purulent discharge
- **CT scan (non-contrast)** — gold standard imaging
- **Lund-Mackay scoring system** — quantifies disease severity on CT

### Treatment Algorithm
**Step 1: Medical Management**
- Intranasal corticosteroid sprays (daily, minimum 8-12 weeks)
- Saline irrigation (high-volume, low-pressure preferred)
- Short course oral steroids (for CRSwNP acute exacerbation)
- Antibiotics — culture-directed when purulent discharge present

**Step 2: Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (ESS)**
- Indicated when maximal medical therapy fails
- FESS (Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery) — gold standard
- Balloon sinuplasty — minimally invasive alternative for select cases

**Step 3: Biologics (for refractory CRSwNP)**
- Dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα) — FDA approved for CRSwNP
- Omalizumab (anti-IgE) — FDA approved for CRSwNP
- Mepolizumab (anti-IL-5) — FDA approved for CRSwNP

## 3. HEARING LOSS (สูญเสียการได้ยิน)

### Types
- **Conductive** — outer/middle ear pathology (otitis media, otosclerosis, cerumen impaction)
- **Sensorineural (SNHL)** — inner ear or cochlear nerve damage
- **Mixed** — combination of both
- **Central** — CNS auditory processing disorder

### Age-Related Hearing Loss (Presbycusis)
- Most common cause of SNHL
- Affects **~25% of adults aged 55-64** and **50% of those ≥75**
- Bilateral, symmetric, progressive high-frequency loss
- Associated with cognitive decline and dementia risk

### Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL)
- **Definition**: ≥30 dB hearing loss over 3 contiguous frequencies within 72 hours
- Incidence: 5-27 per 100,000 annually
- **Medical emergency** — requires urgent evaluation

### Clinical Practice Guideline (AAO-HNS)
1. Distinguish SSNHL from conductive hearing loss using tuning fork tests
2. Obtain audiogram within 14 days of symptom onset
3. Offer **oral corticosteroids** as initial therapy
4. Offer **intratympanic steroid injection** as salvage therapy
5. Do NOT routinely obtain CT or MRI at initial presentation (unless focal neurologic findings)
6. Counsel patients regarding residual hearing loss and tinnitus management

## 4. TINNITUS (เสียงดังในหู)

### Definition
Perception of sound without an external sound source. May be subjective (heard only by patient) or objective (audible to examiner).

### Prevalence
- **10-15%** of the general population experience chronic tinnitus
- **1-2%** experience severely debilitating tinnitus

### Assessment
- Complete audiologic evaluation
- Tinnitus Handicap Inventory (THI) — standardized questionnaire
- Rule out retrocochlear pathology (acoustic neuroma) with MRI if asymmetric

### Management
1. **Education and counseling** — most important first step
2. **Sound therapy** — masking devices, environmental enrichment
3. **Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT)** — habituation-based approach
4. **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — evidence-based for distress reduction
5. **Hearing aids** — when concurrent hearing loss present
6. **Avoid** ototoxic medications when possible

## 5. EPISTAXIS (เลือดกำเดาไหล)

### Classification
- **Anterior** — 90% of cases, Kiesselbach's plexus (Little's area)
- **Posterior** — 10%, sphenopalatine artery branches

### Risk Factors
- Dry climate, low humidity
- Nasal trauma/nose picking
- Anticoagulation therapy (warfarin, DOACs)
- Antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel)
- Hypertension
- Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT)
- Nasal tumors

### Management Algorithm
**Step 1: First Aid**
- Lean forward, pinch soft nose for 15-20 minutes
- Apply topical vasoconstrictors (oxymetazoline spray)

**Step 2: Anterior Packing**
- Nasal tampon, Merocel, or Rapid Rhino device
- Absorbable packing (Surgicel, Floseal) for coagulopathy patients

**Step 3: Posterior Packing**
- Foley catheter balloon or posterior nasal pack
- Requires hospital admission for monitoring

**Step 4: Surgical Intervention**
- Sphenopalatine artery ligation (SPAL) — endoscopic approach
- Anterior ethmoidal artery ligation
- Angiographic embolization — for refractory cases

## 6. OBSTRUCTIVE SLEEP APNEA — ENT Perspective

### ENT Examination in OSA
- **Nasal assessment** — septal deviation, turbinate hypertrophy, polyps
- **Oral cavity** — Mallampati score (I-IV), tonsil size (0-4+)
- **Drug-Induced Sleep Endoscopy (DISE)** — dynamic airway evaluation

### Surgical Options for OSA
1. **Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP)** — palatal surgery
2. **Tonsillectomy** — first-line in children with OSA
3. **Septoplasty ± Turbinate Reduction** — improve nasal airway
4. **Tongue base procedures** — transoral robotic surgery (TORS)
5. **Maxillomandibular Advancement (MMA)** — most effective surgical option
6. **Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulation (Inspire)** — implantable device

## 7. VERTIGO AND VESTIBULAR DISORDERS (เวียนศีรษะ)

### Common Causes
1. **BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)** — most common (50% of peripheral vertigo)
   - Brief episodes triggered by head position changes
   - Diagnosis: Dix-Hallpike maneuver
   - Treatment: Epley maneuver (canalith repositioning)

2. **Vestibular Neuritis / Labyrinthitis**
   - Acute onset severe vertigo lasting days
   - Usually viral etiology
   - Treatment: Vestibular suppressants acutely, then vestibular rehabilitation

3. **Meniere's Disease**
   - Triad: episodic vertigo + fluctuating hearing loss + tinnitus
   - Low-salt diet, diuretics as first-line
   - Intratympanic gentamicin/steroid for refractory cases

4. **Acoustic Neuroma (Vestibular Schwannoma)**
   - Unilateral SNHL + tinnitus ± disequilibrium
   - MRI with gadolinium for diagnosis
   - Observation vs. surgery vs. stereotactic radiosurgery
