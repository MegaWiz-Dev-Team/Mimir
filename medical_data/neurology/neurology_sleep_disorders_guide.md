# Neurology & Brain Disorders — Clinical Reference Guide
## Sources: NCBI Bookshelf, WHO, PubMed Central, AAN Guidelines

---

## 1. SLEEP AND NEUROLOGICAL DISORDERS

### Overview
Individuals suffering from neurological disorders commonly experience sleep abnormalities. Common patterns include:
- More fragmented sleep leading to more awakenings
- Reduced total sleep time
- Decreased REM sleep
- Sleep impairments usually worsen as the disease progresses

---

## 2. ALZHEIMER'S DISEASE

### Sleep Disturbances
- Occur in up to **45%** of patients
- **Sundowning** — increased confusion, agitation, and wandering in late afternoon/evening
- Disrupted circadian rhythms
- Higher risk of obstructive sleep apnea
- Sleep fragmentation may accelerate cognitive decline

### Pathophysiology
- Degeneration of suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — master circadian clock
- Reduced melatonin production
- Beta-amyloid accumulation — impaired glymphatic clearance during sleep

### Management
- Light therapy (bright light exposure during day)
- Melatonin supplementation (low dose, 0.5-5mg)
- Sleep hygiene optimization
- Environmental modifications for safety

---

## 3. PARKINSON'S DISEASE

### Sleep Disturbances (60-98% of patients)
1. **REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD)**
   - Acting out dreams — punching, kicking, shouting during REM
   - May precede motor symptoms by **years to decades**
   - Strong predictor of alpha-synucleinopathy
   - Over 80% with isolated RBD eventually develop Parkinson's or related disorder

2. **Excessive Daytime Sleepiness (EDS)**
   - Affects 15-50% of PD patients
   - Dopaminergic medications may contribute
   - "Sleep attacks" — sudden irresistible sleep episodes

3. **Insomnia**
   - Sleep maintenance insomnia most common
   - Related to motor symptoms (rigidity, tremor), nocturia, medication effects

4. **Restless Legs Syndrome**
   - 2-3x more common in PD patients
   - May overlap with or be difficult to distinguish from akathisia

### Treatment Considerations
- Low-dose clonazepam or melatonin for RBD
- Optimize dopaminergic timing for motor-related insomnia
- Modafinil for excessive daytime sleepiness

## 4. EPILEPSY

### Sleep-Epilepsy Relationship
- Seizures can disrupt sleep architecture
- Sleep deprivation can **lower seizure threshold**
- Some seizure types are predominantly sleep-related:
  - Frontal lobe epilepsy — nocturnal seizures
  - Benign epilepsy with centrotemporal spikes (BECTS)
  - Electrical status epilepticus in sleep (ESES)

### Sleep-Related Epilepsy Syndromes
- **Nocturnal Frontal Lobe Epilepsy (NFLE)**
  - Brief motor seizures during NREM sleep
  - Hyperkinetic movements, vocalizations
  - May be misdiagnosed as parasomnia

### Antiepileptic Drugs and Sleep
| Drug | Effect on Sleep |
|------|----------------|
| Phenobarbital | ↑ Sleepiness, ↓ REM |
| Carbamazepine | Improves sleep continuity |
| Valproate | ↑ Stage 1 NREM |
| Lamotrigine | May improve sleep quality |
| Levetiracetam | Variable — insomnia or somnolence |
| Gabapentin/Pregabalin | Improves sleep, ↑ slow wave sleep |

## 5. STROKE

### Sleep-Disordered Breathing and Stroke
- OSA is both a **risk factor for** and **consequence of** stroke
- Prevalence of OSA after stroke: **50-70%**
- Central sleep apnea — common in acute stroke phase

### Post-Stroke Sleep Disorders
1. **Insomnia** — most common (30-60% of stroke survivors)
2. **Hypersomnia** — especially with thalamic/brainstem strokes
3. **Sleep-disordered breathing** — worsened by pharyngeal muscle weakness
4. **Circadian rhythm disruption** — reduced light exposure during hospitalization

### Impact on Recovery
- Poor sleep quality negatively affects:
  - Motor recovery and rehabilitation outcomes
  - Cognitive recovery
  - Mood and quality of life
  - Risk of recurrent stroke

### Management
- CPAP for post-stroke OSA — may improve recovery outcomes
- Sleep hygiene and environmental optimization
- Treat comorbid depression
- Early mobilization and daytime activity

## 6. HEADACHE DISORDERS

### Migraine
- **Prevalence**: 12% of the general population (18% women, 6% men)
- Bidirectional relationship with sleep:
  - Sleep deprivation triggers migraine
  - Migraine disrupts sleep
  - Sleep can terminate migraine attacks

### Sleep-Related Headaches
1. **Hypnic Headache** — "alarm clock headache"
   - Exclusively occurs during sleep
   - Age onset typically >50 years
   - Responds to caffeine, lithium

2. **Cluster Headache**
   - Often occurs during REM sleep
   - Circadian and circannual periodicity
   - Associated with OSA in some patients

3. **Morning Headache**
   - Common in OSA (12-18% prevalence)
   - Bilateral, pressing quality
   - Resolves with CPAP treatment

## 7. PERIPHERAL NEUROPATHY AND SLEEP

### Diabetic Neuropathy
- Painful neuropathy worse at night — disrupts sleep onset
- Associated with RLS
- Affects 30-50% of patients with longstanding diabetes

### Treatment for Neuropathic Pain Affecting Sleep
- **Gabapentin/Pregabalin** — dual benefit: pain relief + sleep improvement
- **Duloxetine** — SNRI, helps pain and comorbid depression
- **Amitriptyline** — low dose, sedating, analgesic properties
- **Capsaicin topical** — local pain relief without systemic sedation

---

## 8. MULTIPLE SCLEROSIS (MS)

### Sleep Problems in MS
- **Insomnia**: 40-50% prevalence
- **Fatigue**: most common and disabling symptom (75-90%)
- **RLS**: 2-3x higher than general population
- **Nocturia**: due to bladder dysfunction
- **Pain**: neuropathic pain, spasticity disturbing sleep

### CSF Hypocretin
- Hypocretin/orexin levels may be reduced during MS relapses
- Can present with narcolepsy-like symptoms
- Hypothalamic lesions may cause symptomatic narcolepsy

---

## 9. NEURODEGENERATIVE DISEASES — Sleep as a Biomarker

### Early Detection Through Sleep Changes
- **RBD** → Alpha-synucleinopathy (Parkinson's, Lewy Body Dementia, MSA)
  - 10-year conversion rate: >80%
- **Circadian disruption** → Alzheimer's disease risk
- **Sleep fragmentation** → General cognitive decline risk

### Glymphatic System
- Brain waste clearance system active primarily during **NREM sleep**
- Clears beta-amyloid and tau proteins
- Disrupted sleep → impaired clearance → accelerated neurodegeneration
- Target for therapeutic intervention (sleep optimization)
